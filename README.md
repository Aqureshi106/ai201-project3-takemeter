# TakeMeter — AI201 Project 3

Fine-tuning a DistilBERT text classifier on labeled Hacker News quantum computing posts and comparing it to a zero-shot Groq baseline.

---

## Community

**Source:** Hacker News (news.ycombinator.com) — a tech-focused link aggregator and discussion forum used heavily by software engineers, researchers, and startup founders.

Hacker News is a good fit for discourse classification because its quantum computing content spans a genuinely wide quality spectrum in a single place. At one end: substantive comments that walk through surface code thresholds, explain why qubit count is a misleading metric, or critique a paper's experimental setup. At the other end: breathless "this changes everything" headlines, stock-pump-adjacent announcements, and speculative timeline predictions posted with no supporting reasoning. Between those extremes sits a large middle category of community debate — field comparisons, career questions, company roadmap arguments, skeptical commentary about hype — that is neither purely technical nor purely hype.

This three-way variation makes the classification task non-trivial: a vocabulary-matching approach won't succeed because the same terms ("quantum supremacy," "breakthrough," "milestone") appear in all three labels. The meaningful signal is *how* the post uses those terms — whether it asserts, explains, or debates.

Data was collected via the Hacker News Algolia Search API (`hn.algolia.com/api/v1/search`), which is fully open and requires no authentication. The scraper (`scrape_reddit.py`) queries terms across story and comment types and deduplicates by post ID.

---

## Labels

Three labels are used. Full definitions and edge-case decision rules are in `planning.md` Sections 2 and 3.

| Label | Definition |
|---|---|
| `hype` | A claim about QC capabilities, impact, or timeline with no grounding in technical evidence or reasoning. Announcement-driven, reactive, or financially motivated. The defining feature is the *absence* of substantive reasoning — not the presence of sensational vocabulary. |
| `technical` | Substantive engagement with QC concepts: algorithms, error correction, hardware architectures, experimental results, or mathematical foundations. The value of the post is in its technical content, not its opinion. |
| `discussion` | Community debate, opinion, field outlook, career questions, or resource requests. The value is the exchange of views. Posts that *criticize* or *contextualize* hype are `discussion`, not `hype` — the label reflects the post's own discourse, not its subject matter. |

The `hype`/`discussion` boundary is the hardest: posts that call out QC overclaiming are not themselves hype. The `technical`/`discussion` boundary depends on whether the post's primary intent is to explain (technical) or to debate and question (discussion), which can be ambiguous when technical vocabulary appears in a discursive frame.

## Dataset

- **Community:** Hacker News quantum computing posts and comments
- **Total examples:** 208
- **Label distribution:** discussion: 89 (43%), technical: 69 (33%), hype: 50 (24%)
- **No single label exceeds 70% of the dataset.**

The target was 70 examples per class. The `hype` class fell short at 50 because Hacker News skews toward technically literate skeptics who critique QC overclaiming rather than produce it. Re-scraping with hype-oriented queries ("quantum breakthrough," "quantum milestone," "quantum stock") reached 50 hype examples, meeting the ≥200 total requirement without any class exceeding 70%.

**Annotation process:** Each post was labeled by reading it against the definitions in `planning.md`. When the text alone was insufficient, the original post was opened via the `hn_link` column. A `pre_labeled` column marks rows where Claude was used to generate an initial suggestion before human review (see AI Usage section). All labels are human-verified. The `notes` column records correction reasoning for rows that were relabeled during the careful annotation pass. Three difficult annotation decisions are documented in `planning.md` Section 3b.

## Label Agreement (Human vs. AI Pre-labels)

The stretch goal calls for inter-annotator reliability between two independent human labelers. No second annotator was available, so this section reports the next-best measure already built into the annotation workflow: agreement between Claude's initial pre-labels and the final human-verified labels in `dataset.csv`.

Every row was pre-labeled by Claude (claude.ai) in batches of ~30 using the definitions from `planning.md`, then reviewed by the human annotator before the label was finalized. Claude's original suggestion is reconstructed in `label_agreement.csv` (`ai_label`); where the human accepted the suggestion unchanged, `ai_label` equals `human_label`. The 20 overrides are recorded in the `notes` column and flagged with `corrected=1`.

Rebuild the comparison file and recompute metrics with:

```bash
python build_label_agreement.py
python label_agreement.py
```

**Scope note:** This measures human–AI agreement, not inter-annotator reliability between two people. It is included because the full annotation pass (208 examples, well above the 30-example minimum) provides an auditable reliability check on the labeling process — and the correction patterns directly motivated the edge-case rules in `planning.md`.

### Agreement Metrics

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Examples compared | 208 |
| Accepted without change | 188 / 208 |
| **Percent agreement** | **90.4%** |
| **Cohen's κ** | **0.856** |
| Human corrections | 20 |

Cohen's κ = 0.856 falls in the "almost perfect" range (Landis & Koch, 1977). High agreement is expected on clear-cut posts; the 20 corrections concentrate on borderline cases where Claude applied surface keyword matching rather than the discourse-intent rules.

Claude's pre-label distribution was nearly balanced (discussion: 70, technical: 68, hype: 70) because batches were drawn from mixed search queries. The final human distribution shifted toward more `discussion` (89) and less `hype` (50) after the correction pass — reflecting Hacker News's skeptical tone and the rule that posts criticizing hype are `discussion`, not `hype`.

### Correction Analysis

All 20 human corrections involved label-boundary disputes already identified as hard in `planning.md`:

| AI → Human correction | Count | Share of corrections |
|---|---|---|
| `hype` → `discussion` | 17 | 85% |
| `technical` → `discussion` | 2 | 10% |
| `hype` → `technical` | 1 | 5% |

**Pattern 1 — Keyword-triggered hype labels (17 cases, `hype` → `discussion`)**

Claude's pre-labeling batches were drawn heavily from hype-oriented search queries ("quantum breakthrough," "quantum milestone," "quantum stock"). Claude consistently labeled posts as `hype` when the text contained milestone/breakthrough/advantage vocabulary — even when the post's actual discourse was skeptical commentary, expert contextualization, or historical description.

Representative corrections:

| Post (truncated) | AI label | Human label | Why the human overrode |
|---|---|---|---|
| "HSBC publishes yet another claim of quantum advantage that isn't" | hype | discussion | Skeptical meta-commentary calling out a claim — not producing hype |
| "Why Google's Quantum Supremacy Milestone Matters — By Scott Aaronson" | hype | discussion | Expert contextualization with reasoning; subject is hype but post is not |

| "Reports that a 'breakthrough' claim did NOT replicate" | hype | discussion | Opposite of hype — debunking a claim |

These 17 corrections led directly to the edge-case rule in `planning.md` Section 3: *"Posts that criticize or contextualize hype are `discussion`, not `hype`."* That rule was added before the careful relabeling pass and is also the rule added to the Groq baseline system prompt after reviewing where the zero-shot model would likely fail.

**Pattern 2 — Technical vocabulary, discursive intent (2 cases, `technical` → `discussion`)**

| Post (truncated) | AI label | Human label | Why the human overrode |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Ask HN: … textbook/course recommendations (Trotter decomp, stabilizer…)" | technical | discussion | Resource request; technical terms name topics, not explain them |
| "Commenter asking for timeline estimates on drug-discovery applications" | technical | discussion | Field-outlook question despite NMR/drug-discovery vocabulary |

These match planning.md edge case 2: technical vocabulary used as framing for a debate or resource question → `discussion`.

**Pattern 3 — Real result behind hype headline (1 case, `hype` → `technical`)**

| Post (truncated) | AI label | Human label | Why the human overrode |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Information stored in the phase is far more protected against errors…" (paper quote) | hype | technical | Quotes a specific experimental result about phase-based qubit storage; substantive technical content |

This is planning.md edge case 3 (short posts): Claude read the "milestone" framing; the human opened the link and found a real physics result worth labeling `technical`.

### Impact on Final Labels

All 208 final labels in `dataset.csv` are human-verified. The 20 corrections above are the only rows where Claude's suggestion was overridden; the remaining 188 were accepted as-is. Correction reasoning for all 20 is in the `notes` column of `dataset.csv` and in `label_agreement.csv`.

The correction rate (9.6%) and its concentration on the `hype`/`discussion` boundary confirm two things: the label definitions are workable when edge-case rules are applied, and AI pre-labeling is useful for speed on clear cases but unreliable on posts where keyword overlap with hype queries does not reflect discourse intent — the same boundary the fine-tuned model later struggled with most.

### Error Analysis

The fine-tuned model produced 11 wrong predictions out of 32. All 11 fell within a 0.34–0.37 confidence range — barely above random chance (0.33 for three classes). The model is not confidently wrong; it is near-uniformly uncertain at every label boundary it fails at.

---

**Error 1 — Academic framing masking a hype post**

> *"Researchers at University of Waterloo reach milestone in quantum standardization"*
> True: `hype` — Predicted: `technical` (confidence: 0.34)

**Which labels are confused?** `hype`→`technical`. Two of the four hype errors go in this direction. The pattern is directional: the model is not confusing hype and technical randomly — it specifically reads institutional/academic framing as a signal for `technical`.

**Why is the boundary hard?** The label `hype` is defined by the *absence* of supporting reasoning, not by sensational vocabulary. This headline uses neutral institutional language ("Researchers," "University of Waterloo," "standardization") — vocabulary that appears in credible technical news posts too. The hype signal is what isn't there: no mechanism, no number, no finding stated. The model learned hype ≈ sensational vocabulary, but the actual definition is hype ≈ ungrounded claim, which is harder to learn because it requires detecting the absence of something.

**Labeling problem or data problem?** Not a labeling problem. By the planning.md definition, an institutional headline with no technical follow-through is `hype` regardless of its tone. It is a training distribution problem: the dataset's hype examples likely skew toward obviously sensational framing ("revolution," "breaks everything"), leaving the model without examples of academically-worded hype to learn from.

**What would fix it?** More training examples of hype posts that use credible, neutral language — academic press releases, institutional milestone announcements without technical substance. The model needs to learn that "Researchers at [institution] reach [milestone]" with no follow-through is still hype.

---

**Error 2 — Technical vocabulary in a skeptical dismissal**

> *"Honestly, I'm skeptical that this even demonstrates quantum supremacy. Like I said in a different comment in this thread, all they did was let the device run for some time on whatever nonsense wa..."*
> True: `discussion` — Predicted: `technical` (confidence: 0.37)

**Which labels are confused?** `discussion`→`technical`. Two errors go this direction. The mechanism is vocabulary leakage: technical terms in a non-technical discourse frame pull the prediction toward `technical`.

**Why is the boundary hard?** "Quantum supremacy" and "device" are terms the model associates with the `technical` class. But this post contains no analysis — it is a dismissal referencing prior thread context ("Like I said in a different comment"), using pronouns with no referent in the current text. Without the parent comment, the model cannot tell this is a skeptical reply rather than an explanation. The post also contains raw HTML entities (`&#x27;`) from the Algolia API's unprocessed comment text, which tokenize differently from the characters they represent.

**Labeling problem or data problem?** Not a labeling problem. Skeptical dismissal with no technical content is clearly `discussion`. The fix has two parts: HTML-decode the training corpus before tokenization (cheap, affects all comment-type rows); and include more discussion training examples from skeptical commentary threads to show the model that technical vocabulary in a dismissive/hedging register = `discussion`.

**What would fix it?** HTML preprocessing and more critical-commentary training examples. The hard case to demonstrate explicitly is: technical vocabulary + dismissive or hedged stance = `discussion`.

---

**Error 3 — Conversational register overriding technical content**

> *"But for anyone who wonders why I've been obsessing for years about the need to design efficiently verifiable near-term quantum supremacy experiments: well, this is why! We're now deeply into..."*
> True: `technical` — Predicted: `discussion` (confidence: 0.37)

**Which labels are confused?** `technical`→`discussion`. This is the dominant confusion pair: 5 of 11 errors go this direction, accounting for 5 of the 6 wrong technical predictions. It is the primary failure mode.

**Why is the boundary hard?** This post is written by a leading researcher (Scott Aaronson) in an excited, first-person register ("I've been obsessing," "well, this is why!"). The framing is conversational and personal. But the content — the design of efficiently verifiable quantum supremacy experiments — is a substantive technical position, not a field-outlook opinion. The model responds to surface register rather than content depth. Enthusiasm and first-person voice are not `discussion` signals; absence of technical substance is. The model has not learned to separate these two dimensions.

**Labeling problem or data problem?** Not a labeling problem — the post is advancing a technical argument about experimental design. It is a training distribution problem: technical posts in the dataset are likely drawn disproportionately from dry, formal explanations, while technical posts written in an excited or conversational style by domain experts are underrepresented. The model learned technical ≈ formal/explanatory tone.

**What would fix it?** More training examples of technical posts that read conversationally: expert comment-thread responses, rapid-fire technical back-and-forth, domain experts writing quickly under a deadline. The model needs training signal for the case: enthusiastic/personal tone + substantive technical content = `technical`.

### What the Model Captured vs. What Was Intended

The label definitions in `planning.md` are built around *discourse intent*: what is the post trying to do, and does it ground its claims in reasoning? The fine-tuned model learned something simpler and shallower.

**What the model appears to have captured:**
- Vocabulary associated with each class. Technical vocabulary (quantum supremacy, error correction, qubit, threshold) → lean toward `technical`. Conversational or hedging language → lean toward `discussion`. Absence of both → lean toward `hype`.
- Register (formal vs. informal). Formal explanatory tone → `technical`. First-person or question-framing → `discussion`.
- Majority-class bias. When uncertain, default to `discussion` (the most common training label at 43%).

**What was intended:**
- `hype` should be identified by the *absence* of grounding — a claim made without supporting mechanism or evidence, regardless of whether the vocabulary is sensational or institutional.
- `technical` should be identified by whether the post's *primary value* is in explaining, analyzing, or computing — regardless of the author's tone or register.
- `discussion` should be identified by whether the post's *primary value* is in the exchange of views it generates — including posts that use technical vocabulary as scaffolding for an opinion.

**The gap:** The model learned surface proxies (vocabulary, register) for labels that were defined by deeper properties (reasoning structure, discourse intent). This is expected from 208 examples: a fine-tuned DistilBERT cannot learn that "absence of reasoning = hype" from a small training set when the surface vocabulary of hype posts varies enormously. Similarly, it cannot learn that "formal tone does not imply technical intent" without enough counter-examples showing conversational technical posts.

**What the model overfit to:** The vocabulary distribution of each class in the training set. Hype posts that happened to use sensational language trained the model to associate sensationalism with `hype`, rather than to associate *ungrounded assertion* with `hype`. Technical posts that happened to be written in a dry explanatory style trained the model to associate formal register with `technical`, rather than to associate *substantive content* with `technical`.

**What it missed:** The reasoning structure of posts — whether claims are supported, whether the post explains or merely asserts. This is the defining feature of the taxonomy, and it requires a model that has been trained on enough examples to generalize from surface vocabulary to the underlying discourse pattern. 208 examples, split across three classes, is insufficient for that generalization in a 66M-parameter model.

## Confidence Calibration

The notebook reported confidence scores for the 11 misclassified examples. All fall within a narrow band of 0.34–0.37 — barely above the 0.33 random-chance floor for a three-class problem.

| Confidence range | Examples | Wrong predictions in range |
|---|---|---|
| 0.33–0.40 | 11 known errors | 11 / 11 (all errors) |
| > 0.50 | unknown (not captured) | 0 (no error exceeded 0.37) |

**What this means for calibration:** The model never made a wrong prediction with confidence above 0.37. This is actually a useful property — the errors are clustered at low confidence, meaning a confidence threshold would serve as a reliable filter. A deployment threshold of 0.50 would have flagged all 11 wrong predictions for human review while letting through the 21 correct predictions (which presumably scored higher, though individual scores were not captured for those rows).

**To run a full calibration analysis**, add this cell to the Colab notebook after training:

```python
import torch
import os
from transformers import pipeline

DEVICE = 0 if torch.cuda.is_available() else -1

# The trainer saved the model in this specific subdirectory
model_path = "/content/takemeter-model/checkpoint-30"
print("Loading from:", model_path)

cal_pipe = pipeline(
    "text-classification",
    model=model_path,
    top_k=None, # This ensures we get scores for all classes
    device=DEVICE,
)

results = []
for _, row in test_df.iterrows():
    # The pipeline with top_k=None returns a list of dictionaries
    outputs = cal_pipe(row["text"][:512])[0]
    
    scores = {}
    for item in outputs:
        key = item["label"]
        # Map internal LABEL_X to human-readable labels if necessary
        if key.startswith("LABEL_"):
            idx = int(key.split("_")[1])
            key = ID_TO_LABEL[idx]
        scores[key] = item["score"]

    predicted = max(scores, key=scores.get)
    confidence = scores[predicted]
    true_label = ID_TO_LABEL[row["label_id"]]
    correct = (predicted == true_label)
    results.append((confidence, correct))
    print(f"{'✓' if correct else '✗'}  conf={confidence:.3f}  pred={predicted:12s}  true={true_label}")

print("\nCalibration buckets:")
for lo, hi in [(0.33, 0.50), (0.50, 0.70), (0.70, 1.01)]:
    bucket = [(c, a) for c, a in results if lo <= c < hi]
    if bucket:
        acc = sum(a for _, a in bucket) / len(bucket)
        print(f"  {lo:.2f}–{hi:.2f}  n={len(bucket):2d}  accuracy={acc:.3f}")
```

**Key calibration finding:** All model errors are low-confidence (≤ 0.37). In a deployed system where predictions below 0.50 are withheld for human review, this classifier's errors would be caught automatically — at the cost of routing roughly one-third of all predictions to a human queue (since the model rarely exceeds 0.50 on ambiguous examples). A threshold between 0.45 and 0.55 would be the natural operating point.

## Deployed Interface

A Gradio interface is included in `gradio_demo.py`. It accepts a post as free text, runs it through the fine-tuned model, and returns the predicted label, confidence score, and a score breakdown for all three classes.

**To run in Colab** (after training, in a new cell):

```python
!pip install -q gradio
import gradio as gr
import os, torch
from transformers import AutoTokenizer, AutoModelForSequenceClassification

for _p in ["/content/takemeter-model/checkpoint-30", "/content/takemeter-model", "/content/finetuned_model"]:
    if os.path.isdir(_p):
        MODEL_PATH = _p
        break
else:
    raise FileNotFoundError("Model not found. Run !ls /content/ to check the saved path.")

print("Loading from:", MODEL_PATH)
ID2LABEL = {0: "discussion", 1: "technical", 2: "hype"}
_tokenizer = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained("distilbert-base-uncased")
_model = AutoModelForSequenceClassification.from_pretrained(MODEL_PATH)
_model.eval()

def classify_post(text):
    if not text.strip():
        return "Enter a post above."
    inputs = _tokenizer(text[:512], return_tensors="pt", truncation=True)
    with torch.no_grad():
        probs = torch.softmax(_model(**inputs).logits, dim=-1)[0]
    scores = {ID2LABEL[i]: probs[i].item() for i in range(3)}
    predicted = max(scores, key=scores.get)
    lines = [f"Predicted:  {predicted}", f"Confidence: {scores[predicted]:.3f}", "", "All scores:"]
    for label in ("discussion", "technical", "hype"):
        bar = "█" * int(scores[label] * 30)
        lines.append(f"  {label:12s} {scores[label]:.3f}  {bar}")
    return "\n".join(lines)

demo = gr.Interface(
    fn=classify_post,
    inputs=gr.Textbox(lines=6, placeholder="Paste a Hacker News QC post here…", label="Post text"),
    outputs=gr.Textbox(label="Result", lines=10),
    title="TakeMeter — QC Post Classifier",
    description="Fine-tuned DistilBERT · labels: hype · technical · discussion",
    flagging_mode="never",
    examples=[
        "Google's new quantum chip solves in minutes what would take classical computers 10,000 years. This changes everything.",
        "The surface code threshold theorem: below ~1% physical error rate, each concatenation layer exponentially suppresses logical errors. The 1000:1 overhead is the real bottleneck.",
        "Is quantum computing going to break Bitcoin? Some say 10 years, some say never. What do people here actually think?",
    ],
)
demo.launch(share=True)
```

## Spec Reflection

**One way the spec helped:** The explicit edge-case rules in `planning.md` Section 3 provided a consistent resolution mechanism during annotation. The rule "posts that criticize or meta-comment on hype are `discussion`, not `hype`" resolved the single most common borderline case in the dataset: Hacker News posts written by skeptics who reference QC hype as their subject. Without this rule written down before annotation began, those posts would likely have been annotated inconsistently — some labeled `hype` because of the subject matter, some `discussion` because of the tone. Having the rule in writing meant every instance was resolved the same way, which is especially important for a class boundary the model ultimately still struggles with.

**One way the implementation diverged:** The spec planned for 70 examples per class — a balanced 210-row dataset. The actual dataset has 89 discussion / 69 technical / 50 hype (208 rows, imbalanced at 43/33/24%). The hype class fell short because the HN community's character works against it: technically literate users critique and contextualize QC claims rather than amplify them, so even hype-targeted search queries ("quantum breakthrough," "quantum milestone") return mostly skeptical commentary. Re-scraping with seven hype-oriented query terms only reached 50 examples. Trimming to 50/50/50 = 150 rows was rejected because it violated the ≥200 requirement, so the imbalance was accepted. The imbalance contributed to the model's over-reliance on `discussion` as the default label.

## AI Usage

**Instance 1 — Pre-labeling assistance (annotation phase)**

Claude (claude.ai) was used to generate initial label suggestions for batches of roughly 30 posts at a time. The prompt included the full label definitions from `planning.md` and asked Claude to assign one of `hype`, `technical`, `discussion` and briefly state the reason. Every suggestion was reviewed before being written to the CSV. Rows carry `pre_labeled=1` to mark that Claude generated the starting suggestion; the final `label` column is always human-verified. Where Claude's suggestion was overridden, the `notes` column records the correction reason (see Label Agreement section).

What was changed: 17 of Claude's `hype` labels were overridden to `discussion` during the careful relabeling pass. Claude's pre-labeling was generated using keyword-matched hype-query results, so many posts that discussed or criticized hype were initially labeled `hype` by keyword association. Reading each post against the edge-case rule — "posts that criticize or meta-comment on hype are `discussion`" — corrected these. The `notes` column in `dataset.csv` records the correction reason for each overridden row.

**Instance 2 — Groq baseline system prompt**

Claude was used to draft the `SYSTEM_PROMPT` for the zero-shot Groq classifier. The initial draft used the label definitions as written in `planning.md` and included one example post per class. The draft was then edited to add the sentence: "Posts that criticize or contextualize hype are discussion, not hype" — this rule was not in the initial draft and was added after reviewing which boundary the zero-shot model was most likely to fail on (the same hype/discussion edge-case that caused the most annotation errors). Without this addition, the Groq model would likely have labeled many critical-commentary posts as `hype`, mirroring Claude's pre-labeling errors.

**Instance 3 — Error pattern analysis**

After the fine-tuned model produced 11 wrong predictions, Claude was used to systematically check six pattern hypotheses against the 11 cases — counting exactly how many errors each pattern applied to. The agent was asked to report which patterns held, which were weak, and which should be discarded, and to surface any unlisted pattern in the data. Two results were used directly in the README: the finding that all 11 errors fall within a 0.34–0.37 confidence band (suggesting underfitting rather than miscalibration), and the unlisted finding that three errors involved comment-thread excerpts whose pronouns and references only make sense with the parent post. One hypothesis (sarcasm as a pattern) was explicitly discarded because it applied to at most 2 of 11 cases.
